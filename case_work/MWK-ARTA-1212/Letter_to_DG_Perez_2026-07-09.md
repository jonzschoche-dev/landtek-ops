Dear Director General Perez and Head of Public Assistance:

I write to you not, in the first place, as a complainant, but as a citizen who is genuinely confused — and who is turning to the Authority for honest guidance.

I do not come to you about a single resolution. I come to you about a **pattern** — about the treatment I have received across **four separate complaints**, brought over many months, against different officials of the same Municipality, over different ordinary government services. Each complaint has now been recommended for closure. And in every one, the conclusion has been the same word: **"no prima facie"** violation of R.A. No. 11032.

I act as Attorney-in-Fact for an heir of a family that, decades ago, donated public roads to the Municipality of Mercedes, Camarines Norte, on the express condition that those roads carry the donor family's name. My principal depends on the government to do ordinary things — to enact the ordinance the Municipality already accepted, to release certified copies of tax declarations, to issue an official receipt, to answer a written request. These are not exotic demands. They are counter-level services, several of them chartered with published processing times measured in minutes. Across four complaints, they have been delayed, and at points blocked, in ways that have directly hindered my duty to preserve and manage the estate's property. With respect, that is precisely what red tape looks like to the citizen standing at the counter.

And yet — after four complaints, after acceptance and review by the Complaints division, after conversations with front-line Public Assistance staff, and even after speaking with a member of the legal staff of your own Office of the Director General — the outcome has not varied. Four times the door has been opened with courtesy, and four times it has closed at the same threshold. That consistency is what moves me to write. One dismissal I could accept as the fortunes of a single case. But **four complaints, four officials, four services, one identical result** is no longer about the facts of any one matter. It begins to look like a posture.

I want to be fair, and to say plainly what has gone right. I am sincerely grateful for the referrals the Authority has made, including to the Office of the Ombudsman. Those actions matter, and I do not take them for granted. But they sharpen rather than settle my confusion: if the Authority saw enough in this record to refer officials elsewhere, it is harder still to understand why, complaint after complaint, it found nothing within R.A. No. 11032 to act upon itself.

And here is the heart of my confusion. If the true obstacle is that I lack the legal personality to bring these complaints, or that these are simply not R.A. No. 11032 matters at all, then surely that threshold determination could — and should — have been made once, at the very first intake, sparing everyone the months that followed. Instead, each of the four was accepted, docketed, briefed, and processed through several levels, only to be turned away at the same threshold at the end. A gate that only closes after the traveler has walked the entire road, four separate times, is not really a gate. It is delay wearing the costume of due process.

I understand that other remedies — before the Civil Service Commission, the Office of the Ombudsman, or the courts by way of mandamus — may be pursued at the same time, and I am prepared to pursue them. I have already carried these matters, in turn, to the Office of the President. But this is the **Anti-Red Tape Authority.** If a citizen can bring four good-faith complaints about four denied government services and receive, four times over, the same threshold refusal to apply the very law the Authority exists to enforce, then the question I respectfully put to you is a simple one: **if R.A. No. 11032 is not upheld across a record like this one, what is the promise of this Authority for?**

I have invested months of good-faith effort in these proceedings precisely because I believed — as the Authority itself advertises — that ARTA would use its powers to bring relief to citizens like my principal and to help restore fairness in the system. I do not doubt the sincerity of that public promise. My worry is narrower, and I think fair: that a citizen can be met with every courtesy at the counter — the warm reception, the referrals, the careful language — and still walk away, four complaints later, with **nothing the law was written to give.** A velvet glove is a kindness only if there is a hand inside it that finally closes on the wrong.

I therefore ask, sincerely, one of two things. Either **apply R.A. No. 11032 to this record** as I believe the law requires — or **explain to me plainly why it does not,** across these four matters and in terms I can understand and act on — so that I am not left to conclude, against my own hope, that the promise and the practice do not meet.

Thank you for your time, your service, and your consideration. I remain hopeful, and I remain willing to furnish any further information the Authority may need.

Respectfully yours,
